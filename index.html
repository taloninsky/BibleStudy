Revelation Study

Revelation 5

Coming right off the last chapter, where we saw God the father on the throne, what is the next vision all about? you will notice that there are several repeated references, but there are many key things to keep in mind.


Rev 5:1 “Then I saw in the right hand of Him who was seated on the throne a scroll written within and on the back, sealed with seven seals.”


  • Random


    • It makes me think about the creatures and their eyes all over and on the inside in Rev 4:8, but not sure if that connection is at all relevant.

    • Another interesting connection is in Is 30, where Is 30:1 says: “Ah, stubborn children,” declares the Lord, “who carry out a plan, but not mine, and who make an alliance, but not of my Spirit, that they may add sin to sin;” I’m thinking of the plan or alliance represented in a scroll/document...


  • Others who may have seen the scroll:


    • The scroll was written on the front and the back, like Ezek 2:9-10, which says that there were written on it words of lamentation, mourning and woe.

    • I wonder if it is the same scroll that was referred to in Dan 11-12, specifically in Dan 12:4, but this seems strange to me. If it is, then the words themselves are sealed? Perhaps this is the understanding that is sealed (considering that we have the book of Daniel). Maybe the book that is being referred to is the same book, and we no longer have it, God has it. This seems less likely to me, but I’m just putting a stake in the sand.

    • Isaiah also had a vision that was “sealed” even to Isaiah himself (Isaiah 29:11). More broadly, this takes place in the context of at least the full chapter (Isaiah 29). So maybe that is the sense in which this physical copy was sealed: that it cannot be understood. Just a quick note, it is very interesting what God says in Is 29:13, that “their fear of Me is a commandment taught be men,” indicating that a true fear of the Lord is taught be the Lord (either formally or experientially, I assume).


  • Title Deed Idea


    • Jeremiah signs and seals title document, with things on the inside and the outside that in Jeremiah 32:6-15, might be a type of prototype of this action, some have suggested that the scroll in question was a title deed for the future (since this is right before an invasion).

    • Along the lines of the title deed, it should be noted that land was not sold in perpetuity, but rather for the maximum lease of 50 years (Jubilee, technically 49 years, 7x7, since the 50th year was the year it was given back). Lev 25:23-28.

    • Similarly, Paul talks about the creation being subjected to futility (the title currently belonging to Satan, but being subjected to Satan by God’s curse in Genesis 3). Rom 8:20-23.

    • The temptation of Jesus by Satan in Matt 4:8-10, Satan’s tempts Jesus by a shortcut of not having to “pay” for the deed, and that he would just “give” it to Him, if Jesus worshiped Satan.


  • Military elements


    • Ps 110:1, “The LORD says to my Lord: ‘Sit at My right hand until I make Your enemies a footstool for Your feet’ ” which indicates that their are military elements to be taken care of.

  • “As though it had been slain.” This is really interesting. This is the new perfected body of Christ, the Lion of Judah, who will rule, and yet He appears to John as though He had been slain. Certainly this points to the sacrificial nature of Christ’s life and death, I wonder if it might also point to the eternal marks of His sacrifice...


    • the Greek word is ‘esphagmenon’ which translates to ‘had been slain’ (other translations slaughtered). Related words which have the same root are ‘Slaughter’, ‘massacre’, ‘Victim’, ‘sacrifice’. The Greek form indicates a completed action with ongoing effects. Obviously, His sacrifice has the ongoing effect of our redemption, but I also wonder about the ongoing effects on the Lamb. The text does say “...standing as if it had been slain...” so it is alive and upwardly mobile. This is not a surprise, but I’m exploring the literal text. This is related to His mediating work (Heb 7:23-25). He can continue to intercede because 1) His sacrifice was perfect and complete, 2) because He rose from the dead (He is standing).

Revelation 5:12 “saying with a loud voice, ‘Worth is the Lamb who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and might and honor and glory and blessing!’ ”


  • "Worthy” is ‘Axion’ in Greek. Having definitions: worthy, deserving, valuable, proper, in keeping with or corresponding to what is expected. These English words are made from it:


    • Axiom - which is often used in philosophical or mathematical contexts to mean “self-evident truth” or “that which is thought worthy or fit”

    • Axiomatic - obviously similar to Axiom, but further considered universally accepted without needing further explanation. As in, ‘assumed to be true’.

    • Axis - think of the x axis and the y-axis from when you learned to graph a line in math class. though primarily derived from the latin word axis, meaning “pivot” or “axle”, it is also derived from the Greek “worthy” or “fitting”

    • Axial - related to axis, but also having the further implications, such as in of a scientific or mechanical context to describe structures or movements related to a central axis… such as the axial vs radial movements of a tire on a car. It points to centrality.


  • “is” - this is present tense. It is a tensed verb in English, but even more in Greek. In Greek it is the third-person, singular. It connects the object (the Lamb) with its current quality or state (worthy). It is also in the active voice, which means the object (the lamb) is actively performing the action of being or existing in this state. In Greek, there is a quality called “Aspect” and this verb has the imperfect aspect, meaning the action or state is continuous or ongoing. i.e. the Lamb continues to be worthy.

  • “who was slain” ~~~> in Greek, the words do not exist for “who was” so a more literal translation reads something like “Worth is the Lamb slain”. Looking into more of the Greek implication, the form of the word slain is “to esphagmenon” which is described as the perfect passive participle meaning “having been slain” or “who was slain”. Perfect Tense means that action or event occured in the past, but has ongoing significant or results in the present. Passive voice, the Lamb is the one who received the action of being slain, not the one performing the action. i.e. it was not a suicide. Lastly, it is a participle, so it specifies why the Lamb is considered worth --> because of the perfection of the Lamb and His sacrifice, that is what makes Him worthy.
